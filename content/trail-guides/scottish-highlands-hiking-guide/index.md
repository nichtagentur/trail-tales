# Hiking the Scottish Highlands: Best Routes and Wild Camping

The Scottish Highlands stretch across 25,000 square kilometers of raw mountain terrain where ancient Caledonian forests meet windswept ridges that have tested hikers for centuries. I've watched summer storms roll across Rannoch Moor in minutes and camped beside lochs so still they perfectly mirror the surrounding peaks—this landscape demands respect but rewards those who come prepared.

Scotland's unique access laws make these mountains remarkably welcoming. Unlike most of Europe, you can legally camp almost anywhere under the Scottish Outdoor Access Code, transforming multi-day routes into true wilderness expeditions without the permit bureaucracy found elsewhere.

## What Makes Scottish Highlands Hiking Different from Other Mountain Regions?

The Highlands operate on their own weather system. I've experienced four seasons in a single afternoon on Ben Nevis—sunshine, sleet, fog, and gale-force winds before lunch. The Atlantic's proximity means conditions shift rapidly, and summer temperatures rarely exceed 20°C (68°F) even in valleys.

That marine climate creates year-round hiking opportunities, though each season presents distinct challenges. Winter (November-March) brings snow to peaks above 600 meters, requiring ice axes and crampons for serious routes. May through September offers the longest daylight—up to 18 hours in June—but also brings the infamous midges.

These tiny biting insects swarm in calm, damp conditions from late May through August, particularly bad in the western Highlands around Glen Coe and Fort William. A head net costs £8-15 and saves considerable misery during evening camp setups. Smidge or Avon Skin So Soft provide the most effective deterrents.

Scotland's Right to Roam legislation permits wild camping on most unenclosed land, provided you follow leave-no-trace principles. This freedom means you can pitch beside remote lochs or high on mountain plateaus without booking anything in advance—though popular areas near Loch Lomond now have seasonal camping restrictions due to overuse.

## Which Highland Routes Deliver the Best Multi-Day Experiences?

The West Highland Way remains Scotland's most famous long-distance trail for solid reasons. This 154-kilometer route from Milngavie (near Glasgow) to Fort William crosses diverse terrain—lowland forests, Loch Lomond's eastern shore, the haunting expanse of Rannoch Moor, and finally the dramatic Devil's Staircase into Glen Coe.

Most hikers complete it in 6-8 days, wild camping or using bothies (free mountain shelters) along the route. The section through Glen Coe offers the most spectacular scenery, where the trail threads between Buachaille Etive Mor's 1,022-meter pyramid and the Three Sisters' steep-sided valleys.

Water sources appear regularly, though treating all stream water remains essential—sheep and deer populations mean giardia risk exists throughout the Highlands. A lightweight filter like the Sawyer Mini (£25) handles this easily.

For experienced mountain walkers, the Cape Wrath Trail provides Scotland's most challenging long-distance route. This 380-kilometer wilderness traverse from Fort William to Cape Wrath requires genuine navigation skills—much of the route follows no marked path, crossing trackless peat bogs, river fords, and remote mountain passes.

Budget 15-20 days for the full route. Several sections require careful timing around river crossings, which become dangerous after heavy rain. The Kinloch Hourn to Shiel Bridge section particularly demands attention, with multiple knee-deep fords in normal conditions.

The Skye Trail offers 128 kilometers of dramatic coastal and mountain hiking across the Isle of Skye. The Trotternish Ridge section provides the most memorable walking, with the Old Man of Storr's 55-meter pinnacle and the Quiraing's otherworldly rock formations. Ferry access from Mallaig costs £5.45 per passenger, with regular CalMac services.

## What Summit Routes Challenge Experienced Highland Hikers?

Ben Nevis (1,345 meters) stands as Britain's highest mountain and attracts 150,000 summit attempts annually. The standard Mountain Track (formerly called Tourist Path) climbs 1,352 meters over 17 kilometers return, starting from the Glen Nevis visitor center (parking £4 per day).

Don't underestimate this route despite its popularity. The upper section above the Red Burn crosses boulder fields where the path disappears into mist, and the summit plateau features dangerous cliffs on three sides. Navigation skills and proper gear matter here—hypothermia claims lives most summers when underprepared walkers get caught in deteriorating weather.

Allow 7-9 hours round trip. Summit conditions typically run 10°C cooler than Fort William with significantly stronger winds. Pack waterproofs regardless of valley weather—I've learned this lesson repeatedly. Quality rain gear makes the difference between enduring Scottish weather and suffering through it, similar to the gear requirements for [Iceland's Laugavegur Trail](/trail-guides/laugavegur-iceland-guide/).

The Aonach Eagach ridge in Glen Coe provides Scotland's most technical mainland scramble. This 2-kilometer knife-edge traverse connects two Munros (Scottish peaks over 914 meters) with sustained Grade 2 scrambling and severe exposure. The Crazy Pinnacles section requires careful route-finding where one wrong move leads to serious consequences.

Complete this route in good weather only, preferably after several days of dry conditions when the rock provides reliable friction. Budget 8-10 hours for the full horseshoe including Am Bodach and Sgorr nam Fiannaidh. Several mountain guides in Glencoe village (£180-220 per day) offer led ascents.

The Cuillin Ridge on Skye represents the ultimate Scottish scrambling challenge—12 kilometers linking all eleven Munro summits with sustained technical climbing up to Grade 3. Most climbers take 2-3 days, bivvying on the ridge. Only attempt this with solid rock climbing experience and a climbing partner.

## Where Should You Wild Camp for the Best Highland Experiences?

Glen Etive offers exceptional wild camping with minimal restrictions. Drive the single-track road from Glen Coe toward Loch Etive to find countless flat pitches beside the River Etive, with Ben Starav's 1,078-meter bulk dominating the skyline. The river provides fresh water, and no facilities mean true wilderness camping.

Several pull-offs between the A82 junction and Gualachulain accommodate small campervans, though these spots fill quickly in summer. Continue further south for more isolation—I've found perfect pitches near Coileitir with zero other campers even in July.

Loch Ossian sits at the remote heart of the Central Highlands, accessible only by the Corrour Station (Britain's highest railway station at 408 meters) or a 13-kilometer walk from Rannoch Station. The estate permits wild camping around the loch's shores, offering stunning sunsets reflecting off the water with zero light pollution.

The youth hostel at Loch Ossian costs £25 per night if weather turns serious, providing emergency shelter. Most wild campers pitch along the northern shore, where flat grass pitches overlook the loch toward the Ben Alder massif.

Glen Affric provides ancient Caledonian pine forest camping near Loch Affric, 15 kilometers west of Cannich. The forest track reaches several perfect pitches where you can camp beneath Scotland's native pines—trees that have stood for 300+ years. Red deer frequently graze near dawn, and crossbills call from the canopy.

This glen connects to the magnificent Glen Shiel over the 945-meter Bealach an Sgairne pass, enabling multi-day backpacking loops. Water sources appear constantly, and the forest provides some wind shelter—valuable in this exposed landscape.

## How Do You Prepare for Unpredictable Highland Weather?

Layering systems prevent both hypothermia and overheating during steep ascents. I base-layer with merino wool (Icebreaker or Smartwool 200-weight), mid-layer with a lightweight fleece, and carry a down jacket for stops and camps. A waterproof jacket and pants remain essential year-round—not optional equipment.

Highland weather requires more robust rain gear than many hikers expect. A jacket with 20,000mm waterproof rating and fully taped seams handles the horizontal rain typical here. My Arc'teryx Beta AR has survived five Scottish seasons, though solid options exist at lower price points. The principles apply anywhere wet weather dominates, as discussed in this [comprehensive rain gear guide](/gear-reviews/rain-gear-hiking-guide/).

Wind resistance matters as much as waterproofing. I've experienced 80 kph gusts on exposed ridges during "normal" summer conditions. A hood that actually stays on your head (not blowing back) and pit zips for ventilation during ascents prove their worth repeatedly.

Pack liner bags (even with a waterproof pack) keep sleeping gear and spare clothes dry during inevitable rain. I use a 30-liter dry bag inside my 65-liter pack for all overnight kit. One wet sleeping bag in these conditions creates a miserable experience with limited recovery options.

Navigation skills and equipment aren't optional. GPS units and phone apps help, but carry paper maps (OS Landranger 1:50,000 scale) and a proper compass. I download offline maps through ViewRanger or OS Maps before departing, since phone signal disappears rapidly once away from main roads.

Emergency shelters weigh under 200 grams and provide lifesaving protection if weather deteriorates badly. The SOL Emergency Bivvy costs £15 and fits in a jacket pocket. Mountain Rescue teams regularly extract hypothermic walkers who thought they'd "just tough it out."

## What Gear Essentials Handle Highland Conditions?

Tent selection depends on whether you prioritize weight or weather protection. My Hilleberg Anjan 2 GT (2.1 kg) handles every storm the Highlands throw at it, though the MSR Hubba Hubba (1.7 kg) suffices for summer trips with decent weather forecasts.

Four-season tents aren't necessary outside winter, but look for: aluminum poles (fiberglass breaks in wind), full-coverage fly that reaches the ground, and strong pegging points. Testing guy lines before the first Highland storm saves midnight tent failures.

Sleeping bags rated to at least 0°C serve for summer camping given the elevation and maritime climate. I carry a Rab Neutrino 200 (comfort 3°C, limit -3°C) for three-season use. Ground insulation matters significantly—a sleeping pad with R-value above 3.0 prevents heat loss into cold Highland ground.

Water capacity needs less attention here than desert regions—reliable sources appear constantly. A single 1-liter bottle and a collapsible 2-liter bladder cover most needs. The Platypus Platy bottles weigh almost nothing and roll up when empty.

Cooking systems face unique challenges. Gas canisters perform poorly in cold, wet conditions compared to liquid fuel stoves. The MSR WhisperLite Universal runs on multiple fuel types and handles wind better than integrated canister systems, though it's heavier (360g vs 200g for a Pocket Rocket).

Trekking poles reduce knee strain on steep descents and aid stability crossing streams—both common Highland situations. They also speed tent pitching for trekking pole shelters and help probe bog depth before committing your weight.

Midges aside, Scottish Highlands hiking rewards adventurous spirits with landscapes largely unchanged since the last ice age. Those ancient glaciers carved these U-shaped valleys and sculpted these peaks—now you get to explore them armed with modern gear and Scotland's remarkably progressive access laws. Just remember: the weather will test you, the midges will annoy you, but the memories will outlast both.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: When is the best time for hiking the Scottish Highlands?

May through October offers the most reliable conditions, with May-June providing longer daylight (up to 18 hours) and fewer midges before their peak season. September offers excellent walking with fewer crowds and spectacular autumn colors, though days shorten noticeably. Winter hiking (November-March) requires winter mountaineering skills and equipment for routes above 600 meters.

### Q: Do you need permits for wild camping in the Scottish Highlands?

No permits are required for wild camping in most of the Scottish Highlands under the Scottish Outdoor Access Code. You can camp on unenclosed land provided you follow leave-no-trace principles, stay no more than 2-3 nights in one spot, and camp away from buildings and roads. Notable exceptions include restricted areas around Loch Lomond (seasonal camping bans March-September) and some private estates during deer stalking season (August-October).

### Q: How difficult is the West Highland Way compared to other long-distance trails?

The West Highland Way presents moderate difficulty with 154 kilometers of well-maintained path and approximately 4,400 meters of cumulative elevation gain. The terrain varies from easy lochside walking to challenging mountain passes like the Devil's Staircase (550-meter climb). Most reasonably fit hikers complete it in 6-8 days, making it more accessible than technical routes like the Cape Wrath Trail but more demanding than England's flat canal paths.

### Q: What's the biggest mistake first-time Highland hikers make?

Underestimating weather severity and changeability causes most problems for first-timers. Many arrive during rare sunny spells and assume that's typical, then get caught unprepared when conditions deteriorate rapidly. The second common mistake is inadequate navigation skills—relying solely on phone GPS without backup maps, compass, and knowledge to use them. Scottish mountains demand respect regardless of season, with hypothermia possible even in summer when wet, cold, and windy conditions combine.
